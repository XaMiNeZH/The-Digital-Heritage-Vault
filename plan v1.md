### The Narrative Arc (The Story)

Your professor wants empathy and persuasion. Your team already has the right instinct — **technology as villain turned hero**. Here's how to structure it:

**Act 1 — The Villain is Born** _(Problem / Evidence)_

> "Every 40 days, a language dies. Not because people forgot. Because the world moved too fast."

Open with emotional weight. Real data, visceral. Make the audience _feel_ the loss before they understand it intellectually. A language dying = a library burning. A craft disappearing = a century of knowledge gone overnight.

**Act 2 — The Wound** _(Argument — why this matters)_

> "We didn't lose these cultures. We outpaced them."

Technology — smartphones, globalization, mass production — is framed as the unconscious villain. Not evil. Just indifferent. Show what flat digitization fails to capture: the muscle memory of a weaver, the rhythm of a potter's hands. Data without soul.

**Act 3 — The Turn** _(The solution enters)_

> "What if the same force that broke it could heal it?"

Technology stops being the villain and becomes the hero. But only if it's designed with the community, not _for_ spectacle. This is where your platform enters — not as a startup pitch, but as a **promise**.

**Act 4 — The Design** _(Technical Visual / App Mockup)_ Show the UI. Keep it simple — 2 or 3 key screens. The professor doesn't want a full app, she wants to see that you _thought about the user_. Who uses this? What do they feel when they open it?

**Act 5 — The Ethical Defense** _(The flaw you acknowledge)_ This is required and most groups will skip it or make it weak. Don't. Pick ONE real risk and defend it seriously:

> "Our biggest risk: we become another platform that _extracts_ culture instead of protecting it."

Then explain your mitigation: community governance, no corporate data mining, communities own their content, they can delete it anytime.

**Act 6 — The X-Factor** _(Physical-Digital Hybrid Interface)_ This is your creative twist — **don't save it for the end as a footnote**. Make it the emotional climax. Pick ONE interface from your brainstorming (the Weaving Wall is the strongest visually and emotionally) and pitch it as the thing that makes everything else possible. "A screen can store a weaving pattern. But only haptic feedback can teach your hands to feel it."

**Act 7 — The Call** _(Close)_ End not with a product pitch but with a moral one. "We're not asking you to fund an app. We're asking you to decide: do we let the next language die on our watch?"

|**The Turn**|Platform introduction. Warm, hopeful shift in color and tone.|
|**The App**|Mobile mockup embedded. 2-3 screens. Clean.|
|**The X-Factor**|Weaving Wall or Memory Theater. One concept, fully explained with a visual/diagram.|
|**The Ethical Defense**|One risk, one mitigation. Transparent and confident.|
|**The Close**|Emotional ending. Not a CTA button. A question or a quote.|

### The App Mockup (What to Show)

Don't try to show the whole app. Show **3 screens** that tell a story:

1. **Home screen** — "What's at risk near you." A map. A living counter.
2. **A Heritage Record** — One language or craft page. Elder photo, audio clip icon, short story, contribution button.
3. **The X-Factor screen** — The haptic/physical interface booking or intro. Something no other app has.

---

### The Ethical Defense (Prepare This Well)

**The Risk:** The platform could become a zoo — cultures preserved for outsiders to consume, not for communities to live.

**The Mitigation:**

- Communities own their content, always
- Sacred/sensitive knowledge is access-controlled by elders, not the platform
- No advertising, no data selling
- Communities can remove their content at any time, permanently
